Robert Tomlinson

Civil War Hero of the 6th Wisconsin Infantry

A Brave Soldier's Sacrifice

Born ~1844, Died June 28, 1864, Andersonville Prison

Biography

Robert "Bob" Tomlinson was born around 1844 in Ohio to Thomas and Mary Tomlinson, English immigrants who settled in Diamond Bluff, Wisconsin. At ~17, he enlisted in the Union Army, joining Company B, 6th Wisconsin Infantry, part of the famed Iron Brigade. Known for his bold spirit, nicknamed "Bombastes Furioso," Robert fought bravely in major Civil War battles before his capture and death at Andersonville Prison at age ~20. He is my great-great-granduncle, brother of my great-great-grandmother.

  • Birth: ~1844, Ohio
  • Hometown: Diamond Bluff, Pierce County, Wisconsin
  • Parents: Thomas Tomlinson (~1801, England), Mary Tomlinson (~1803, England)
  • Death: June 28, 1864, Andersonville Prison, Georgia

Military Service

Robert enlisted on May 10, 1861, in Company B, 6th Wisconsin Infantry, the "Prescott Guards," and mustered in on July 16, 1861. As part of the Iron Brigade, he fought in key Eastern Theater battles:

  • Gainesville (Brawner’s Farm), August 1862: First major combat, facing Stonewall Jackson.
  • Antietam, September 1862: Fought in the Cornfield, defiantly firing alone.
  • Gettysburg, July 1863: Wounded in shoulder at McPherson’s Ridge/Railroad Cut.
  • Buckland Mills, October 1863: Captured while rushing to join the fight.

Captured on October 19, 1863, at Buckland Mills, Virginia, Robert was sent to Andersonville Prison, where he died on June 28, 1864, likely from disease, and was buried at Andersonville National Cemetery (grave 2582).

Notable Moments

Antietam Defiance (1862)

During the retreat from the Cornfield, Robert shouted, "Not yet! I have a few more cartridges left!" and fired alone under heavy fire, earning his "Bombastes Furioso" nickname (Letter, October 26, 1863).

Gettysburg Bravery (1863)

Wounded in the shoulder, Robert may have repeated his defiance: "God, you ain’t going back are you? Not yet. I still have a few more cartridges left" (Hartwig, 1998). He returned to duty despite his injury.

Buckland Mills Capture (1863)

Despite an unhealed wound, Robert grabbed a pistol and ran to join his regiment’s defense, only to be captured by Confederate cavalry, showcasing his unyielding courage.

Family Context

Robert was part of a family dedicated to the Union cause:

  • John Tomlinson (~1836): Served in Company A, 12th Wisconsin Infantry, likely survived.
  • William Tomlinson (~1843): Served in Company I, 7th Wisconsin Infantry, survived, mustered out 1865.
  • Betsey/Sarah Tomlinson: One is likely my great-great-grandmother, making Robert my great-great-granduncle.

The Tomlinson family’s sacrifices in Pierce County, Wisconsin, reflect their commitment to preserving the Union.

Legacy

Robert Tomlinson’s courage at Antietam, Gettysburg, and Buckland Mills, and his tragic death at Andersonville at age ~20, make him a hero of the Iron Brigade. His story, preserved in letters and histories, honors the sacrifices of my family and thousands of Union soldiers. Visit Andersonville National Cemetery, grave 2582, to pay respects.
